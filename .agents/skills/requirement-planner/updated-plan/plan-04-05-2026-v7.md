## Phase A — Problem framing

### A.1 Define the problem

The broker (BWG) runs an MT5 platform where customers trade FX, commodities, and CFDs. A small but costly minority of traders use technical and policy tricks to extract money in ways that are not honest market participation: latency arbitrage, scalping below the policy minimum, swap arbitrage on hedged books, and bonus or credit abuse. Today, detection is slow and manual. By the time a human spots a pattern, withdrawals may already be paid out. We need an automated assistant that watches every account, scores risk, and surfaces the most suspicious cases to a human reviewer in real time — all without taking final actions on its own.

### A.2 Why solving this matters

- **Direct money lost.** Every successful latency-arb trade is paid out of the broker's pocket, and bonus-abuse rings can drain promotional budgets in hours.
- **License risk.** Regulators expect brokers to demonstrate active monitoring. "We rely on staff to notice" is no longer enough.
- **Spread quality for clean traders.** When abuse is unchecked, the broker compensates by widening spreads, which punishes honest customers.
- **Human review does not scale.** Thousands of accounts × four scans per day is impossible without automation; humans should review the top candidates, not search for them.

### A.3 How we solve it

We use a **Hybrid** approach (locked recommendation from v3 §1.3):

- A **rule engine** computes a 0–100 score from deterministic parameters. This score is the basis of every decision.
- A set of **LLM agents** turn structured data into human-readable text (case summary, customer email draft, CS playbook). They never decide.
- A **human officer** approves every action via a Telegram bot.
- A complete **audit log** records every state change, every prompt hash, and every API call.

Building blocks needed end-to-end: Parameter Extractor → Rule Scorer → LLM Agents → Telegram Bot → Action Executor → Audit Log.

## Phase B — Domain definitions

These are my working definitions of the four illegal-style activities the MVP must detect (PRD §3). Each will be the source of truth for parameter design and LLM prompts.

### B.1 Latency Arbitrage

A trader uses a faster external price feed than ours. They open a position with us **before** our price catches up to the new market price, then close seconds later for near risk-free profit.

- **Example trade.** EURUSD ticks 1.07320 → 1.07338 on a fast external feed at 10:21:00.100. Trader buys 1 lot with us at 10:21:00.250 (our price still 1.07321). Closes at 10:21:14 at 1.07338 = +$17 in 14 seconds.
- **Clean trader for contrast.** Holds positions for minutes to hours, with normal win-rate around 50%, and profit explained by analysis, not by execution timing.

### B.2 Scalping Violation

The broker has a written rule (often per MT5 group) that a trade must be held for at least N seconds (e.g. 60s). The trader deliberately closes faster.

- **Example trade.** Account is in group "Pro-Raw-USD" with min hold = 60s. Trader places 80 trades in a day, 62 of them closed in under 30 seconds, totaling $1,840 of profit.
- **Clean trader for contrast.** May still be a short-term trader, but holds at least the minimum, with mixed P&L.

### B.3 Swap Arbitrage

The trader holds offsetting positions (long one symbol, short a correlated one — sometimes across linked accounts) so the **net market exposure is near zero**, but the position **collects positive swap** every day.

- **Example trade.** Account A goes long 5 lots AUDJPY, account B (same KYC, same IP) goes short 5 lots AUDJPY. Net delta = 0. Both accounts collect positive swap on the long leg minus a smaller charge on the short leg, every night, for 30 nights.
- **Clean trader for contrast.** Carries directional exposure; swap is incidental, not the source of profit.

### B.4 Bonus / Credit Abuse

The trader misuses promotional bonuses or credit. Three common variants:

- **Volume churn:** opening and closing offsetting trades that produce near-zero P&L just to inflate the volume counter.
- **Quick withdraw:** small deposit → claim bonus → request withdrawal before turnover requirement is met.
- **Multi-account ring:** several accounts (same person or small group) sharing IP / device / KYC name to claim the same bonus repeatedly.

- **Example trade.** Customer deposits $10, receives $500 bonus, runs 200 round-trip trades with cumulative P&L of −$3 over 6 hours, then files a $480 withdrawal.
- **Clean trader for contrast.** Genuine deposit, genuine trading P&L over time, withdrawal only after meeting bonus terms.

## Phase D — LLM responsibility

The LLM does not decide anything. It analyses **only** the parameter dictionary computed by the rule engine, and produces text.

### D.1 What the LLM analyses

- Input: a JSON object with `case_id`, `uid`, `risk_type`, `score`, `band`, `window`, `parameters` (numbers only).
- Job: pick the 3–5 parameters that most strongly support the `risk_type` and write a 5-bullet plain-English summary plus a verdict line.

### D.2 How the LLM is constrained

- JSON-only output.
- Max 5 bullets, max 15 words each.
- No invented numbers, no recommended actions, no PII.
- Verdict line must use broker vocabulary (e.g. "Pattern is consistent with latency arbitrage").

### D.3 Optional second-opinion agent

For Medium-band cases (60–74), a separate LLM call may answer one yes/no: _"Do you see anything the rule engine might be missing?"_ Its answer is logged, never acted on. Used to discover new rules over time.

### E.5 How Telegram actions reach the backend

3. Critical actions: send a "Confirm?" reply, wait for second tap.
4. Action Executor calls Alex's write API with the idempotency key.
5. Audit row written **before** the call (intent) and **after** the response (result).

## Phase F — LLM input contract

This phase is the safety boundary that protects customer privacy.

### F.1 The rule

The LLM never receives PII. PII = name, email, phone, postal address, DOB, KYC numbers, raw IP, raw device fingerprint, bank/card numbers, and any free-text written by or about a customer.

### F.2 The whitelist (what may go to the LLM)

`uid`, `case_id`, numeric parameters from v3 §2, symbol names, score, band, window, action names, template ids, language code.

### F.3 How I enforce it in code

- A single function `prepare_llm_payload(case)` is the **only** path to the LLM.
- A unit test scans every LLM call site at build time and fails the build if a forbidden field name appears in the payload schema.
- LLM prompt and output are stored as **hashes** in the audit log; raw text only behind a compliance flag.

### Questions for Alex (Phase F)

20. Will any of your read APIs return **raw IPs or raw device fingerprints**? I would prefer hashed versions on your side so my service never holds the raw values.
21. Do you have a **compliance / data-protection contact** I should sync with on this boundary, so we agree once and avoid surprises?

## Phase G — Email-send flow

The principle: **the LLM addresses the customer by `uid`, never by email; the backend resolves the address at send time.**

### G.1 The three steps

1. **LLM draft.** Inputs: `uid`, `template_id`, `language`, `variables`. Output: `{ subject, body }`. No PII inside.
2. **Officer review in Telegram.** Buttons: Approve & Send / Edit / Cancel. If Edit, the edited text goes straight to send and **never** back to the LLM.
3. **Send.** My service calls `send_email(uid, subject, body, case_id, idempotency_key)`. Your backend resolves `uid → email address` and sends. My service never sees the address.

### G.2 What the audit log stores

`case_id`, `uid`, `template_id`, `prompt_hash`, `output_hash`, `delivery_status` (raw text only behind compliance flag).

24. What email templates are already approved by compliance that I can mirror, so the LLM is filling **slots** rather than writing free-form?
